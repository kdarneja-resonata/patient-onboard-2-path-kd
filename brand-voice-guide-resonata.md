# 1. Audience & Purpose

Who is your primary audience? (patients, providers, or both?)

Describe their demographics, tech comfort level, and health literacy:

There are two primary audiences:

1. Patients who are looking for care options (treatment, tests, therapies) for a serious and/or chronic condition. They are adults of any age and could be men or women. Since they have a fairly serious condition, medical treatment and care is a more prominent feature of their day to day life than it is for healthy adults. They are likely fairly to extremely informed about their condition, but they likely don’t have access to all of the latest available care options due to the complexity of healthcare delivery in the United States. 
    
2. Biopharma companies’ clinical research teams and/or marketing teams.
    

  

What are their main needs, concerns, or pain points when using healthcare technology?

1. Patients need to gain an understanding of all possible care options that could suit them so they can have the best outcomes. In the United States, patients are often stuck between an information shortage (they only know about the care options that their specific doctor is aware of) and a flood of irrelevant information they get by searching the internet (many options may be found, but they have no way of knowing whether they’d be eligible for any given option). This causes further friction between them and their doctor, as they may present a lot of irrelevant information to their doctor who then has to explain why options don’t apply. In addition, patients with serious chronic conditions may have a harder time investing in research about care options as their symptoms may cause them to experience things like fatigue. 
    
2. Biopharma companies desperately want to get their care options to patients they can help. They are also stuck between a kind of information paradox: they can spend enormous amounts of time and money on non-targeted campaigns (like TV commercials) hoping that a patient who might be suitable will see the advertising and then talk to their doctor and then hopefully they are actually suitable for the care option. For treatments that are not yet approved to market, clinical research teams want to perform clinical trials as efficiently as possible in order to make their treatment available to patients who need it. They also face the challenge of finding patients who would be suitable to participate in the trial - which is a very slow and expensive process. 
    

  

What are your core company values?

Seriousness: We aren’t “tech bros”, we’re industry professionals who understand the complexity of the healthcare system and are deeply passionate about helping patients find the care options that are best for them. 

Empowerment: Patients are people, not conditions. The problem isn’t with them, it’s with a healthcare system that’s opaque and extremely difficult to navigate. We want people to have a way to get all their options so they can have the best care possible.

Compassion: Life with a serious chronic condition can be challenging in ways that impact nearly every aspect of life. Getting the right care option as quickly as possible is what people need. We believe it shouldn’t require the leg-work, expense and emotional roller-coaster of trying to find, aggregate and then sift through potential options.

Respect: While most people may not be medical experts, they are knowledgeable and capable of making informed decisions if we give them the information they need.

# 3. Tone by Context

Voice is consistent, but tone adapts to context. Describe how your tone should shift in these situations:

  

Onboarding new patients:

Empowering, optimistic, hopeful and reassuring that we’re a tool that they can trust to get them real care options. They can be confident that we understand what they are looking for and that we take their privacy extremely seriously.

  

Explaining medical information:

Accessible, but never “dumbing down”. We do not want to sound like a gimmicky marketing tool trying to sell a treatment; we also don’t want to sound patronizing or like we are explaining something to a child. The tone should be professional and serious, but not using a language that only medical professionals could decipher. 

  

Error messages or technical issues:

Short, pithy, actionable but calm. Nothing cute or technical or exclamatory. 

Good: “Your matches will be ready soon. We’ll email you to let you know.”

Bad: “No matches! Sorry 🙂”

  

Success confirmations:

Short, pithy and reassuring that they can trust us.

Good: “Thank you. Consent form completed.”

Bad: “Thanks!! You’re the best!”

Bad: “Done”

  

Appointment reminders:

Brief, calm, not personal, friendly. 

Good: “Reminder: There are still 2 steps to do to complete enrollment. Click here to start.”

Bad: “You need to complete steps 6 and 7 or enrollment will not be completed.”

Bad: “Hey! Just reminding you your enrollment isn’t complete yet!”

  

Billing and payments:

Brief, calm, not personal, friendly.

What makes your brand voice unique compared to other healthcare apps?

We are healthcare people, not silicon-valley tech bros; we aren’t mass-marketing a gimmick, we’re providing a sophisticated matching service that actually solves a patient problem. We are not trying to persuade patients to buy something.

  

When people interact with your brand, what should they feel?

That they are dealing with a serious, trustworthy partner who has a deep understanding of the healthcare system and the challenge they are facing. They should feel optimistic and hopeful and competent and empowered and supported.
