# Dorothy Martinez — Patient Onboarding v2 Usability Review
**Date:** 2026-05-13
**Prototype:** patient-onboarding-v2-prototype.html (semi-conversion pivot)
**Synthetic User:** Dorothy Martinez (68, rural Nebraska, refractory generalized MG 18yr, low tech, daughter-dependent)

---

## 1. First impressions

My daughter Linda pulled this up on the tablet for me on Sunday after church. She'd seen something about it from one of those online MG groups she belongs to, and she said, "Mom, just look at it with me, you don't have to do anything."

The first thing I saw was a green dot up in the corner — a little blinking thing — and then the words *"Care options for people who've tried things that haven't worked."* I'll be honest with you, that stopped me. That line right there. Because that's me. That's exactly me. After eighteen years of this disease, after the rituximab and the prednisone and the eculizumab that I couldn't take because of the meningitis thing, and the PLEX and the IVIG and all of it — that sentence is the first one in a long time that didn't feel like it was written for somebody else.

The text was big enough. I could read it without my reading glasses on, which surprised me. The page wasn't busy. It wasn't full of stock photos of smiling people in white coats. There was a green button that said "See if we can help" and a smaller line underneath that said *"Takes about 2 minutes. No medical records needed to start."* And I said to Linda, "Two minutes. They say two minutes. We'll see."

What I said to her, exactly, was: *"This doesn't look like one of those things trying to sell me something."* That's a compliment. Most of what we get in the mail or pops up on the computer looks like somebody is trying to sell me something.

But I'll tell you what I was also thinking — and I want them to hear this — I was thinking, *they're going to ask me about my records. They always do. And mine are at the university hospital and the local hospital and the home health agency and I don't even know where else, and I can't go pulling all that together. I just can't.* So I was already half waiting to be told to stop.

## 2. Step-by-step walkthrough

**Screen 1 — Landing.** Already talked about it. Pressed the green button. Linda did the pressing, actually. Her finger is steadier than mine on a good day, and today is a middling day.

**Screen 2 — Indication check.** It asked me what condition I'm working on. I picked Myasthenia Gravis. There was a little dropdown thing. I told Linda, "Just pick MG, that's what I have." She did. The page didn't take forever to load, which I appreciate. I've used some of those hospital websites where you press a button and you can go make a cup of coffee before anything happens.

**Screen 3 — Quiz question one.** This one asked me how long I'd been diagnosed. I said "more than 10 years" because eighteen is more than ten. Easy. The options were big and tappable. Linda didn't have to zoom in.

**Screen 4 — Quiz question two.** This was about what I'd tried. Now, this is where I got nervous, because I have tried *a lot.* But the screen had a list of treatments with little check boxes, and you could check more than one. Rituximab, IVIG, PLEX, prednisone, pyridostigmine, all the ones I've done. I checked off six of them. Linda said, "Mom, you've been through it, haven't you?" And I said, "You knew that already."

There was also a check box that said something like *"Tried more than 3 things that didn't work well enough."* I checked that one. It felt good to check it. It felt like, finally, someone is asking the right question. Most forms only let you tell them what you're on *now.* They don't give you a place to say, "And here are the four other things that failed me first."

**Screen 5 — Quiz question three.** This was about how I'm doing now. Whether I'm stable, having flares, in crisis, that kind of thing. I picked "frequent flares" because that's been the truth this past year. The rituximab isn't holding me the way it used to. We're already talking with the specialist about what's next.

**Screen 6 — Preview matches.** And here is where I actually stopped and looked at Linda. Because the screen said *"We see about 4 possible care options that could fit a profile like yours."* And it showed them as kind of fuzzy cards — I could see the names but they were blurry, and there was a little note that said something like *"approximate matches — connect your records for verified results."*

I want to be careful here, because I had two feelings at once.

The first feeling: *Four. Four is not nothing.* Four is more than the zero I sometimes feel like I have. Even one of them being real would be more than I expected.

The second feeling: *Why are you hiding them from me?* The blurriness made me a little uneasy. Linda said, "Mom, they're not really hiding, they just want to make sure you actually qualify before they show the details." I said, "Then why show me anything at all?" She didn't have an answer for that. I think the page is trying to be honest — like, *here's what we see, but we can't promise until we check* — but to me it felt a little bit like a magic trick where the magician shows you the box and won't open it.

**Screen 7 — Complete sign-up.** They asked for my email, my name, a password. Linda made the password. I don't make passwords. They're always too complicated and I forget them. The page said they wouldn't sell my information, and there was a little lock icon. That helped a tiny bit. The button said "Create my account" which is fine.

I will say — and this is important — there was *no* option I could find for "let my daughter sign up alongside me" or "this is a caregiver account." Linda is doing all the tapping. She's the one who'll actually log in next time. But the system thinks it's me. That bothered both of us. She said, "We'll just use my email," and I said, "But then it's not really my account." We didn't know what to do. We used her email in the end.

**Screen 8 — Consent split.** This is the page where it says *"You can connect your records now and get verified matches, or skip that for now and see approximate matches."* Two paths. Path A and Path B, though it didn't quite call them that — it said something like *"Connect now"* and *"Browse for now, connect later."*

I appreciate having a choice. I really do. I almost picked "Browse for now" just to see what it would let me look at without committing. But Linda said, "Mom, if you want to know if any of these are actually real for you, you have to let them see your records." So we did Path A. We picked "Connect now."

But I want to flag — the toggle between the two paths felt like a *decision,* not just a click. The page didn't fully tell me what I was giving up by picking one or the other. It said I could change my mind later, but I wasn't fully sure I believed that. When something says "you can change this later," there's always a catch.

**Screen 9 — Path A authorizations.** Oh, this is where Linda took over completely. There were checkboxes for HIPAA authorization, and a place to pick which hospitals' records they should pull from. The university hospital, the local clinic, the home health agency. Linda knew where mine were. I didn't, not all of them.

The text in this section was smaller than the rest of the website. I noticed that right away. It looked like the lawyer pages. There was a long block of words I didn't read. Linda read some of it. She said, "It's basically saying they'll only use your records to match you to treatments and trials, and they won't sell anything to anyone."

This is the part where, if I'd been doing it alone, I would have closed the tablet.

**Screen 10 — Path A confirmation.** Said something like *"Thanks. We're pulling your records. We'll email you in a couple of days with your verified matches."* I liked that. That was calm. It didn't make a big production of it. It didn't say "WOOHOO!" or anything silly. It just said it was working on it.

**Screen 11 — Path B confirmation.** We didn't end up on this one because we picked Path A, but Linda backed up and showed it to me. It said something like *"Your account is ready. You can browse care options in the meantime. We'll send you reminders when you're ready to upgrade."* The word *upgrade* I didn't love. Felt like I was being upsold to a fancier phone plan.

**Screen 12 — Uncovered indication branch.** Linda showed me what happens if you put in a condition they don't cover. It said something like *"We're not actively matching for this condition yet, but we'll keep you posted."* Calm and honest. Fine.

**Screen 13 — Education explorer.** This was articles and short videos about MG. Some about treatment types, some about clinical trials in general, some about what to ask your doctor. The articles had pictures. The text was big enough. There was a little play button on the videos. I didn't watch one, but I appreciated that they were there. That was actually one of the better pages for me.

**Screen 14 — App home (semi-converted).** This is what you see if you went the Path B route. It still showed me my four approximate matches, but blurred a little, with a button on each that said *"Verify with records to see details."* And it had a section called "Learn while you wait." Honestly, this page was nicer than I expected. It didn't feel like I was being punished for not handing over my records. It felt like, *we'll keep working for you in the background.* I liked that.

I did NOT click into a care option detail page when I was on Path A confirmation — Linda did, on the dev navigator, and showed me the gated version. The page said *"Verify your medical history to see the full details of this option."* That seemed fair, I guess.

## 3. What works

- **The first sentence on the landing page** — naming refractory patients out loud. That is rare. That is what brought me in.
- **Big text. Calm colors.** Not too many things on a screen at once.
- **The 3-question quiz.** Two minutes is not a lie. It was actually fast.
- **The "tried 3 or more things" checkbox** on the quiz. Acknowledging that.
- **Showing approximate matches before asking for records.** I see what they're doing. They're saying, *we exist. We're not nothing. You haven't wasted your time.* That's smart.
- **Path B existing at all.** The fact that I could say "not yet" and still get something — that respects me. Most websites are all-or-nothing.
- **The confirmation pages didn't shout at me.** No exclamation marks. Just "we got it, we'll be in touch."

## 4. What doesn't work

- **No caregiver mode.** Linda is doing this with me, not for me, and the system has no way to know that. We had to use her email and pretend it's mine.
- **The blurred match cards** on the preview page. I get the design choice, but it makes me feel like there's a trick. Show me the names. Tell me you don't know yet if I qualify. Don't make it look fuzzy.
- **The records authorization page** is the dealbreaker page if you don't have help. The text shrinks, the language gets legal, and you suddenly need to know which hospital systems hold your records. For someone like me with records in four places, this was Linda's job entirely.
- **The word "upgrade."** I'm not buying a service. I'm trying to find care for a disease I've had for eighteen years.
- **No voice option anywhere.** I would have liked to be able to listen to the questions instead of read them. On a bad day I can't focus on text.
- **No phone number.** Anywhere. If I get stuck, who do I call? I looked. Linda looked. There's no number. That's a big problem for me.
- **The dev navigator** at the top was confusing, but I know it's just for testing.

## 5. Trust and safety assessment

I'll tell you what earned trust.

The sentence on the front page that named people like me. The calm tone. The lock icon. The fact that nobody said "amazing!" or "revolutionary!" or any of that nonsense. The Path B option — the fact that I could leave without giving them everything and still come back. That tells me they're not desperate. Desperate companies make you commit on page one.

What eroded trust.

The HIPAA authorization page in the small lawyer text. I know it has to exist, but it's a wall. The blurred cards on the preview. The word "upgrade." The fact that I couldn't find a phone number, a real address, the name of a doctor associated with the company, the name of a hospital partner. If I'm handing over my whole medical history, I want to know who I'm handing it to. Are these people backed by a hospital? A university? Are they doctors? I don't know from looking at the page. And that worries me. My specialist would ask me, "Who is this Resonata?" And I'd have to say I don't know.

I'd want to see a name. A face. A hospital affiliation. Something that tells me this isn't a Silicon Valley company gambling with my records.

## 6. Language review

**Words I didn't know:** *indication.* I had to ask Linda. She said, "It means the condition." Why didn't they just say *condition?* Also *semi-converted* showed up once in something I clicked, and that's not a word for me. *Authorization* I know but it's a heavy word.

**Words that felt warm:** *People who've tried things that haven't worked.* *Care options.* *We'll be in touch.* *No medical records needed to start.* Those felt like the company knew who I was.

**Words that felt cold or wrong:** *Upgrade.* *Verify.* *Profile.* I don't have a profile. I have a body and a disease.

**Tone overall:** Respectful. Not patronizing. Didn't talk down to me. Didn't try to be funny or cute, which I appreciate. But also a little bit flat in places — like a form. The front page had heart. The middle pages were a bit cold.

## 7. What's missing

This is the part that matters most to me. Here's what I went looking for and didn't find:

1. **Anything specifically for refractory patients.** The front page promised it. The quiz acknowledged it. But after that, the matches are just "matches." I don't know if any of the four options they showed me are actually for people who've failed rituximab, or if they're the same four they'd show a newly-diagnosed patient. I needed that confirmed somewhere.
2. **Caregiver / daughter access.** Spelled out. Linda needs to be a recognized user, not a workaround.
3. **A phone number.** I need a person I can call. I'm sixty-eight and I'm tired.
4. **Cost.** What does any of this cost me? Will Medicare cover the matches the system found? I saw nothing about money. For me, this matters as much as efficacy.
5. **Treatment burden.** How many appointments? How many infusions? How far would I have to drive? My specialist is already three hours away. I can't add another three-hour drive to my life.
6. **Voice / listen option.** Read it to me.
7. **A "for people who've been doing this a long time" track.** Maybe a separate path for veteran patients. A page that says, *here's what's new since you last looked. Here's what's specifically for refractory cases.*
8. **Palliative and supportive care.** Not every option has to be a new drug. Sometimes I just want to know about symptom management I haven't tried.
9. **Some honesty about the odds.** If there's nothing left, I'd rather be told gently than strung along.

## 8. Overall verdict

Would I complete this on my own? **No.** I would get to the HIPAA authorization page and close the tablet.

Would I complete this with Linda? **Yes — we did, in this walkthrough.** And I'd give it a real chance because of that first sentence on the landing page and because Path B was there as a safety valve.

Would my daughter encourage me to come back? **Probably yes,** if the email actually arrives with verified matches and they don't dump me into a sales pipeline. If the first email is calm and short and says, "Here are your two real matches and here's what to do next," she'll bring it back to me. If the first email is a flashy newsletter or has the word "upgrade" in it, she'll delete it and not tell me.

**The dealbreaker:** If those verified matches come back and none of them are realistic for a refractory patient — if they're all things I've already tried, or things I'm not eligible for — that's it. I won't open the third email. Eighteen years of being a guinea pig has earned me the right to stop quickly when something isn't for me.

**The hope:** If even *one* of those four matches is a real, FDA-approved option I haven't tried, or a trial that's been honest enough to tell me about the placebo arm and the travel and the cost — then I will tell Linda this was worth the Sunday afternoon. And I'll tell my specialist about it at the next visit. That's the only marketing that's ever worked on me.

So: not a "no." Not a "yes." A "we'll see what the email says."
