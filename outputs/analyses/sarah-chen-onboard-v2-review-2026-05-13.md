# Sarah Chen — Patient Onboarding v2 Usability Review
**Date:** 2026-05-13
**Prototype:** patient-onboarding-v2-prototype.html (semi-conversion pivot)
**Synthetic User:** Sarah Chen (52, suburban Chicago, generalized MG 3yr, AChR+, moderate tech)

---

## First Impressions

I'll be honest — by the time I land on a page like this, I'm usually already tired. It's Tuesday afternoon and my eyelids are doing that thing where the left one drops a little earlier than the right, which is how I know I've been on the laptop too long. So my first thought, before I read anything, is: *how long is this going to take?*

The headline says "Your personalized path to better care." Okay. That's a sentence I've read in some form on probably twenty other websites in the last three years. The small green badge at the top — "Built by healthcare experts · HIPAA-protected" — does more for me than the headline does, actually. That's the kind of thing I notice. Two flags that say *this isn't another wellness app from a 25-year-old in Silicon Valley.*

The subhead is what actually made me keep reading: "treatments and clinical trials that fit your specific medical profile — not generic options for everyone with your condition." The phrase "not generic options for everyone with your condition" — that's exactly the problem I have. Every Facebook group post, every Google search, every well-meaning relative who sends me a link — it's all generic. So when something promises me the opposite, I lean in a little.

The right-hand sidebar with "How it works" — three steps, "About 30 seconds," "See your matches," "Go deeper when you're ready" — that calmed me down. The phrase "when you're ready" is doing a lot of work there. It tells me I'm not going to be forced into anything in the first ninety seconds. Good.

What I don't love: the visual. The green is fine — it's clinical, not garish, which I appreciate — but the whole page feels a little flat. There's no image, no illustration, nothing that signals *who* this is for. Am I the right kind of patient? I don't know. The page doesn't show me anyone like me. Just a form.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

**The landing page.** I type my email into the box. The helper text underneath says "We'll save your matches here and send updates only when something new fits your profile." That's good. I've signed up for too many health-tech things that then proceeded to email me three times a week about webinars. The promise of "only when something new fits" is the kind of restraint I want to see.

Then the condition dropdown. I pick "Myasthenia Gravis." The button says "See my care options." I click. I half-expect it to ask me for a password or my insurance card. It doesn't.

**The loading screen** is fine. "Checking your condition coverage…" with the spinning thing. It's a beat — maybe two seconds in real life — but it tells me something is happening on their end. I appreciate that they didn't try to make it cute. No "Stretching out our algorithms!" or whatever. Just plain.

**Quiz Q1 — type of MG.** "Which type of Myasthenia Gravis describes you?" Generalized, Ocular, Juvenile, I'm not sure. Easy. I pick Generalized. The descriptions underneath each option are short and clear — "Affects multiple muscle groups — limbs, breathing, swallowing, and/or eyes." That's how MG is, exactly. No jargon. I notice the "I'm not sure" option includes "we can still show approximate matches based on what you do know" — which is generous and the right thing to say. I'd have used that option, by the way, three years ago.

**Quiz Q2 — antibody status.** "Do you know your antibody status?" Yes — AChR-positive. I know my antibody status because my neurologist drilled it into me, but most people in my MG Facebook group don't, especially the newer ones. The "I don't know" option is here too. Good. What I notice is the helper text: "Your antibody profile changes which treatments and trials are likely to work." That sentence is doing real teaching, and it doesn't talk down to me. It's a fact, presented as a fact. The descriptions under each option — "Most common form (about 85% of generalized MG)" — that's the kind of detail I actually want.

**Quiz Q3 — treatment stage.** "Where are you in your treatment?" This is the question I was waiting for. The options are: Newly diagnosed, On treatment looking for better options, Tried multiple treatments without success, Stable exploring what's new. I would click "On treatment, looking for better options." That's me. The phrasing — "looking for better options" — is honest in a way I don't always feel allowed to be. I'm not in crisis. I'm not refractory. But Mestinon plus prednisone plus monthly IVIG is *a lot,* and I would like fewer things, or a different thing, and I don't know what's reasonable to ask for. The fact that this option exists between "newly diagnosed" and "tried everything" — that's the most validating moment I've had on a health website in a while.

**Preview matches.** This is where I stop being tired and start paying attention. There's a yellow-ish badge that says "Approximate matches based on what you told us." Good. Honest framing. The headline says "Here's what fits your profile." Then I see the numbers: 4 strong matches, 6 potential matches, 3 options we know aren't a fit. That last part — telling me what *isn't* a fit and *why* — that's the thing I've never seen anywhere else. Anywhere. Every other tool I've used just throws every MG trial in the country at me. This one is doing the opposite. *We considered these and they're not for you.* That's real work on their end. I feel it.

The green "Full matches" bucket has Nipocalimab (J&J), Efgartigimod (which I've heard of and discussed with my neurologist), Rozanolixizumab, and a Telitacicept study. Each card has a one-line "why we matched" — "Matches: generalized MG · AChR-positive · on treatment looking for better options." That short sentence is everything. It tells me they understood my answers. It also gives me confidence that if I told them more, they could be more specific.

The yellow "Potential matches" bucket has Eculizumab — which I know is for refractory cases. The card says "Approved for refractory cases — we'd need to confirm prior treatment history to know if you qualify." That's exactly the right note. Not "you can have this!" Not "you can't have this!" Just "we'd need to know more." I trust this more than the green bucket, in a weird way, because it's showing me the uncertainty honestly.

I noticed a save banner near the top: "We saved these matches to your inbox. You can come back to them anytime." I exhale. *I don't have to finish this in one sitting.* For me, that single sentence might be the difference between completing this flow and abandoning it. Today is a moderate-fatigue day. On a bad day, knowing I can stop and pick this up later is huge.

**Complete sign-up.** First name, last name, state, date of birth. Four fields. That feels reasonable. The little note under DOB — "Required by some trials and treatment eligibility" — answers my question before I ask it. Good. I would do this.

**Consent split (the big moment).** "Two ways to move forward." Left card is "Connect your records now," marked "Most precise" with a green badge. Right card is "Connect records later — Keep browsing. Upgrade anytime."

I appreciate that they're not forcing me. I really, really appreciate it. The left side promises "verified matches in about 8 hours" and explains what happens with records. The right side promises that my matches stay accessible and I can upgrade with one click. Both have HIPAA reassurance.

But — and this is honest — I would pick the right side. Not because I don't trust them, but because I'm tired, my daughter is downstairs asking what's for dinner, and I want to think about this for a day before I authorize anyone to pull my records. Even if I know intellectually that's probably the right next step, the fatigue tax on "decide right now" is real for me. The fact that I *can* choose later is the reason I'd come back.

**Path B confirmation.** "You're in. Your matches are saved." Calm. Pithy. "Take your time." I'd close the tab here and feel okay.

**App home (semi-converted).** When I come back — let's say a week later — this is what I'd see. My matches, my condition line at the top ("Generalized MG · AChR-positive · On treatment, looking for better options"), and a yellow banner reminding me these are approximate. There's also a green "upgrade" card: "2 trials in your area are still accepting applications." That's a smart nudge. Not pushy. Specific. *In my area.* That detail makes me actually consider it.

**Care option detail (gated).** I'd click into Nipocalimab to see what it is. The "Why we matched you" list is checkmarks against my quiz answers — generalized MG, AChR-positive, inadequate response to prior therapy. Clear. Then there's a blurred-out section behind a lock icon: "Site-specific eligibility unlocks once we have your medical records." And another for applying. Honest gating. I don't mind it. It tells me exactly what I get if I connect records and exactly what I can still do right now (bookmark, get notified, read).

## What Works

- **The three buckets — Full / Potential / Not a fit.** Telling me what *isn't* for me is unprecedented. I've never seen that. It builds trust faster than any marketing copy could.
- **"Approximate matches" as honest framing.** The yellow badge isn't a downgrade signal — it's a *we're being straight with you* signal. I'd believe everything else this product says because of that one badge.
- **The Path B option.** Letting me stop without losing my matches is the single most important design decision in this whole flow. It respects how I actually live with this disease.
- **The treatment-stage question.** "On treatment, looking for better options" is a real phase of MG life that other tools ignore. Seeing it in writing felt like being seen.
- **The "why we matched" lines on each card.** Short, specific, transparent. They make me feel like a person, not a database row.
- **The save-to-inbox banner on the preview screen.** Permission to leave is what makes me stay.

## What Doesn't Work

- **The visual is too clean.** It looks like a financial product, not a health product. There's no warmth in the imagery — no photography, no human face, no texture. For a condition like MG, where so much of my experience is feeling invisible, this design doesn't quite reach out. It's competent but a little cold.
- **"Connect your records" still scares me, even though they've done the work.** The label "Records Retrieval Authorization (DNAvisit)" on the Path A authorizations screen is the moment I would freeze. DNAvisit? Who is that? Why is the company name in the title? I'd want a one-sentence explanation of who they are and why I should trust them, *before* I see the consent box. Right now it feels like getting handed a contract from a subcontractor I've never heard of.
- **No mention of my doctor anywhere.** Nowhere in this flow does it say "you can share this with your neurologist" or "bring this to your next appointment." That's the single biggest gap. Any decision I make about MG treatment, I make *with* my doctor, not instead of him.
- **The "you're set" Path A confirmation is too breezy for what just happened.** I just authorized a company I'd never heard of to pull my records from Northwestern and Rush. The confirmation should feel a little weightier. A "you can withdraw consent anytime by doing X" reminder. A "here's what we just retrieved" preview later.
- **No side-effect or trade-off information visible anywhere.** The match cards tell me the trial exists and that I match. They don't tell me what the drug *does to you.* Bi-weekly infusions for 52 weeks. Placebo-controlled. That's a serious commitment, and I'd want to know what the side effect profile looks like before I get excited about Nipocalimab. Hiding that until I connect records feels wrong — that's the information I need *before* I decide whether to connect records.
- **"Free for patients" in the trust row is a little jarring.** I know what they mean, but it raises a question they don't answer: *so who's paying?* If the answer is "the trial sponsors" or "the drug companies," I'd actually rather know that upfront. Hiding the business model makes me suspicious.

## Trust and Safety Assessment

Email? Yes, I'd share that. The helper text about restraint helped, and HIPAA badge is visible.

Connect records *today*? No. Not in the first session. I'd want to:
1. Talk to my husband.
2. Look up DNAvisit and figure out who they are.
3. Mention it to my neurologist at my next appointment.
4. Come back in a few days when my brain is fresh.

The fact that the platform lets me do exactly that is why I'd come back. If it forced records upfront, I would close this tab and never return.

The line "We never share anything that identifies you with the companies behind treatments or trials" is good. I'd want to see that repeated on the actual authorization screens, not just on the consent-split screen.

## Language Review

**Words that helped:**
- "Approximate matches based on what you told us" — honest, calm.
- "Not a fit · We're showing you these so you know we considered them — and ruled them out for you." — this is the single best sentence on the site.
- "Take your time." (Path B confirmation) — small phrase, big effect.
- "Connect records to make them precise" — turns the records ask into an upgrade, not a gate.

**Words that didn't help:**
- "Vivacity-MG3," "FcRn," "MG-ADL score" — these are accurate, but they appear before any explanation. I know what FcRn is now because I asked my neurologist. Two years ago I didn't.
- "Semi-converted" — I know I'm not supposed to see this word, but it's all over the dev navigator. The fact that it exists in the product team's vocabulary worries me a little. I'm not a "semi-conversion." I'm a person who didn't share my records yet.
- "Records Retrieval Authorization (DNAvisit)" — the partner name should not be in the title before I know who DNAvisit is.

**Tone that hit:** professional and warm without being syrupy. No "we're so sorry you're going through this." No emojis. No exclamation points. That matters to me. I get enough exclamation points from chirpy wellness apps. This one feels grown-up.

## What's Missing

Things I expected and didn't find:

1. **A "share with my doctor" or "print for my appointment" feature.** Critical. I can't be the only patient who needs this.
2. **Side-effect data on the match cards.** Before I get excited about a treatment, I need to know what it does to people. Weight gain? Infections? Hair loss? Anything visible?
3. **Other patients' experiences.** The "stories from patients" section exists in the uncovered-indication branch but not in the regular flow. Why? Stories matter more in the regular flow than in the consolation prize flow.
4. **Cost / insurance information.** Especially for the FDA-approved drugs. Eculizumab is famously expensive. Knowing what insurance typically covers would change which match I'd actually pursue.
5. **A way to come back to my session if I'm interrupted.** The save-to-inbox thing helps, but I want a "you're 60% through" indicator. Especially during quiz Q3, I forgot the progress bar at the top because it's so subtle.
6. **A larger font option.** On a bad eyelid day, the body text is fine but the option meta lines ("Open enrollment · Sites in 14 states") are small.
7. **A "compare these two" tool.** If I'm choosing between Efgartigimod and Rozanolixizumab, I want them side by side. The bucket view doesn't let me do that.

## Overall Verdict

Would I complete this flow? **The first part, yes.** I'd do email, quiz, see my matches, and create my account. I would go down Path B and close the tab feeling cautiously optimistic.

Would I come back? **Yes, if the email follow-up was as restrained as the page promised.** One email per relevant new match. No "we miss you" or "complete your account!" nonsense. If that promise holds, I'd come back within a week.

Would I connect my records eventually? **Probably, yes, but only after talking to my husband and my neurologist.** The 8-hour turnaround time is reassuring. The "withdraw consent anytime" line is what makes it possible.

Would I recommend it to my MG Facebook group? **Yes, with one caveat.** I'd say: "This is the first one that doesn't make you feel stupid. It tells you what's *not* for you, which I've never seen anywhere. Just know that to actually apply to anything, you have to connect your medical records — but you can browse first."

The dealbreaker that almost was: forcing records upfront would have made me close the tab. They got that decision right.

What would make this a 10 instead of an 8 for me: doctor coordination tools, side-effect data on match cards, and a "compare" feature. Add those and I'd tell every MG patient I know.
